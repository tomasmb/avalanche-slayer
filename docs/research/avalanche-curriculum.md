# Avalanche Curriculum Map

## Scope

The app is built to cover the essential knowledge and decision routines that recur across the classic recreational avalanche training pathways in Canada and the United States.

- [Avalanche Canada Training](https://avalanche.ca/training) presents AST 1 as the starting point and describes the progression into Companion Rescue Skills, Managing Avalanche Terrain, and AST 2.
- [AIARE Recreational Avalanche Training](https://avtraining.org/recprogram/) describes a three-part pathway of Avalanche Rescue, AIARE 1, and AIARE 2.

## What the official progressions have in common

Across Avalanche Canada and AIARE, the recurring pillars are:

1. Avalanche fundamentals and hazard vocabulary.
2. Terrain recognition and terrain selection.
3. Forecast use and avalanche-problem interpretation.
4. Trip planning and on-trip decision routines.
5. Group communication and human-factor management.
6. Companion rescue.

This six-part synthesis is an inference from the official course pages above plus the topic structure in [Avy Savvy](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca). It is the curriculum spine used in the app.

## Why terrain gets special emphasis

- [ATES](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca/the-avalanche-terrain-exposure-scale) formalizes the simple / challenging / complex terrain model.
- [The Avaluator Trip Planner](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca/the-avaluator-trip-planner) combines ATES with daily danger to support route appropriateness.
- [Online Trip Planner](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca/online-trip-planner) turns that logic into mapped, practical route planning.

Product inference: terrain is the most stable control lever a recreationist can use before and during a trip, so the app keeps terrain and route filtering at the center of review.

## Why forecast reading is taught as interpretation, not definition memorization

- [North American Public Avalanche Danger Scale](https://avalanche.org/avalanche-encyclopedia/danger-scale/) frames danger ratings and associated travel advice.
- [Avalanche Problems](https://avalanche.org/avalanche-encyclopedia/avalanche/avalanche-problems/) organizes the forecast around type, distribution, likelihood, and size.
- [Avy Savvy](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca) teaches forecast use as a practical planning skill.

Product inference: learners should practice extracting route consequences from forecasts, not only recalling what a danger rating means in isolation.

## Why decision-making and human factors are core, not optional

- [The Daily Process](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca/the-daily-process) provides a structured routine for pre-trip and on-trip decisions.
- [Human Factors](https://avalanche.org/avalanche-encyclopedia/human/decision-making/human-factors/) makes heuristic traps explicit.
- [AIARE 2](https://avtraining.org/aiare-level-2/) explicitly raises the bar on leadership and more complicated terrain decisions.

Product inference: a serious avalanche app has to review judgment habits alongside snow and terrain concepts, because many failures happen in the decision loop.

## Why rescue remains a dedicated mode

- [Avalanche Canada Training](https://avalanche.ca/training) offers Companion Rescue Skills as a standalone refresher and AST add-on.
- [Companion Rescue](https://avysavvy.avalanche.ca/en-ca/companion-rescue) presents rescue as a practiced skill sequence, not background reading.
- [AIARE Recreational Program](https://avtraining.org/recprogram/) separates Avalanche Rescue as its own course element and prerequisite for higher training.

Product inference: rescue skills are too perishable and too high-consequence to leave buried inside general review, so the app exposes a fast rescue queue from the main dashboard.
